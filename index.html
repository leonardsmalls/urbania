  • Hypothesis

    URBANIA - Urban Indoor Agriculture

URBANIA aims to identify existing "food deserts" and propose a solution to help hydrate them via urban and indoor agriculture. This proposition is supported by identifying vacant commercial real estate near areas with limited access to nutritious food and generating figures to justify converting these properties into food production sites. Ideally the food production sites would fall within close proximity to public transportation stops, allowing for further accessibility to affordable, fresh produce.

4 square feet of area can produce a conservative average of 25 lbs of salable produce annually. With 60% of floor space devoted to production, a 4,000 square foot commercial property could potentially yield 15,000 - 18,000 lbs of produce each year. Assuming a generous 100 lbs per person, per year, one 4,000 sq ft space could provide fresh, affordable and accessible produce to over 150 people.

Food Desert

https://www.medicalnewstoday.com/articles/what-are-food-deserts

Food deserts are regions where people have limited access to healthful and affordable food. This may be due to having a low income or having to travel farther to find healthful food options.

Without access to healthful foods, people living in food deserts may be at higher risk of diet-related conditions, such as obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease.

Food Swamp

https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5708005/

Food swamps have been described as areas with a high-density of establishments selling high-calorie fast food and junk food, relative to healthier food options.

  • Conclusion

Urban Indoor Agriculture is a practical solution to providing immediate, affordable relief to areas otherwise considered to be "food deserts".
Production of produce is much faster compared to traditional outdoor methods and the area required to create quantifiable amounts of food is much smaller.

Typical vacant commercial properties are far larger than 4,000 square feet and are otherwise sitting unused. Indoor agriculture production sites could be set-up in one week and relocated just as quickly, allowing for other prospective tenants to occupy the space whenever necessary. Housing produce production efforts would allow utility services to remain in operation and not fall dormant. Security in the area would be increased by not having clusters of vacated properties, nevermind the obvious benefit of providing food to the neighboring community. The prospect of converting unused commercial spaces into agriculture production sites could also provide local employment opportunities.


TLDR; Win/Win!
